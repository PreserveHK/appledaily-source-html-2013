「情況還不算太差」 - 古德明

A Japanese civil servant described what happened in early 1945 when, as a sixth-grade pupil, he was on an evacuation train bound for northeastern Japan and saw the city of Kobe stretching out as a complete wasteland.
Contrary to the violent shock I felt, I unthinkingly said, "It doesn't seem too bad." The middle-aged man sitting next to me asked, "You don't think this is horrible?" I was stuck for an answer, but pointing to some houses left standing in the Rokko Mountains, I insisted, "See, there are still some houses left." At the same time, the thought flashed across my mind, "I wonder if he is a spy." The war leaders of the time justified this crazed psychology by calling it "courageous". Without this psychology, the war could not have been prosecuted.
日本一個公務員憶述一九四五年初的一件事。當時他是小學六年級學生，在往日本東北的一列避難火車上，看見神戶市變成了一片廢墟。
我大為震驚，但卻不假思索，說了句違心之言：「情況看來還不算太差。」坐在我身邊的一個中年男人問：「你認為這並不可怕？」我無詞以對，就指着六甲山上一些未倒的房子，強辯說：「你看，還有些房子沒有倒下。」同時，一個念頭在我心中閃過：「這個人是不是間諜？」當年的戰爭領袖，推許這種荒唐的心理，稱為「勇敢」。沒有這種心理，戰爭是不可能發動的。
